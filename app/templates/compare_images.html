How it works

Step 1

Upload both files

The version you trust and the version in question. Files are hashed on intake and handled under a documented chain of custody.

Step 2

Forensic analysis

SHA-256 comparison, EXIF and metadata examination, perceptual similarity scoring, and manipulation indicators — run the same way every time.

Step 3

Exhibit-ready report

A PDF you can attach to a demand letter, discovery response, or deposition outline — written to be read by judges, adjusters, and opposing counsel.

What the report documents

Cryptographic hash comparison
SHA-256 fingerprints of both files. Identical files match to the character; any alteration — a single pixel — produces a different hash.
Metadata examination
EXIF capture data, device information, timestamps, and software traces — and what's been stripped or rewritten.
Perceptual similarity
Pixel-level and structural scoring of how alike the two images actually are, independent of file format or resizing.
Manipulation indicators
Signals consistent with editing, recompression, or re-capture, stated plainly with their limitations.

Where it earns its keep

Family law: message screenshots that don't match between the parties. Personal injury and insurance: claim photos with edited damage or shifted timestamps. Commercial disputes: documents and images "produced" in a version that differs from the one your client kept.

Evidentix is a triage and documentation tool: it tells you — in writing — whether a piece of digital evidence is worth fighting over, before you spend five figures on a retained examiner.
