It's already happening

The courts are seeing deepfake evidence — and the law is racing to catch up.

In September 2025, a California court dismissed a case with prejudice after the plaintiffs submitted deepfake videos as authentic witness testimony. The judge caught the fakes the hard way — noticing robotic facial movements, looping footage, and metadata claiming an iPhone 6 had captured video only newer hardware could produce. Mendones v. Cushman & Wakefield, Inc., No. 23CV028772 (Cal. Super. Ct. Alameda Cnty. Sept. 9, 2025).

When the plaintiffs sought reconsideration — arguing the court had only suspected, not proven, the evidence was AI-generated — the judge denied it. But the challenge points to a problem every litigant now faces: courts are detecting fakes by suspicion and anomaly-spotting, not by reliable proof of where a file came from.

Legal scholars have flagged the gap. Professor Rebecca Delfino argues that the traditional authentication rule — Federal Rule of Evidence 901 — was never built for AI-generated falsifications, and that courts need new tools to tell real evidence from synthetic. See Rebecca A. Delfino, Deepfakes on Trial, 74 Hastings L.J. 293 (2023).

The “deepfake defense” cuts both ways. Even your authentic evidence can be attacked as a possible fake — forcing you to prove a genuine photo or video is real. The Integrity Certificate documents that proof up front: cryptographic hash, captured metadata, device and location data, and content-credential analysis, recorded at the moment of intake — before anyone has reason to doubt it.

What the Integrity Certificate documents

  • Cryptographic integrity — a SHA-256 fingerprint proving the file hasn't changed since intake.
  • Captured metadata — device, timestamp, and GPS location with an embedded map, read from the file as submitted.
  • Structural fingerprint — a perceptual hash recording the image's visual structure as a forensic property.
  • Content Credentials (C2PA) — analysis of any embedded provenance data, the emerging industry standard for tracing a file's origin.
  • Methodology & limitations — a plain statement of what the certificate establishes and what it does not, written for the record.
